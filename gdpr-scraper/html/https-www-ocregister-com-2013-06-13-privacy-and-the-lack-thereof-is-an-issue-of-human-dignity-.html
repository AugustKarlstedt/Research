Privacy, and the lack thereof, is an issue of human dignity

Share this:

  • Click to share on Facebook (Opens in new window)
  • Click to share on Twitter (Opens in new window)
  • Click to share on Reddit (Opens in new window)
  • Click to print (Opens in new window)
By Orange County Register
June 13, 2013 at 8:52 pm

Sensitivity to privacy is a dominant motif of the Jewish tradition, which links privacy with human dignity and sees its loss as corresponding to a loss of individuality.

A rabbi once asked a student, “Do you want to learn how to be a pious man?”

“Yes, Rabbi,” was the reply.

“A certain man lives in Odessa. Stay with him and you will learn the meaning of piety.”

The student traveled to Odessa and located the man his rabbi had described. He agreed to host the young man and the student settled in to observe the conduct of his pious host. But if the visitor expected lengthy praying each day followed by study throughout the night, he was disappointed. His host proved to be a simple man who arose early, prayed simply and went to work. Upon returning, he would recite evening prayers, eat quickly and go to sleep.

Thus, the student grew no wiser and more bored with each passing day. The student’s quarters were a dim room, where the only window was a small pane set high in a wall. One day, while his host was at work, the restless and curious student climbed onto a table to look out the window. To his dismay, he saw the backyard of a brothel, where lewd activities went on at all hours.

When his host returned that evening, the student asked him, “Tell me, how can a pious person live in proximity to such neighbors? Couldn’t you find a place to live that is not back to back with such an establishment?”

Now it was the man’s turn to be dismayed.

“I’ve lived here for 20 years,” he said, “and not once did it occur to me to look into strangers’ yards to see what they were doing. You, on other hand, are hardly here a few days, and are already climbing on tables and spying on every sinner in neighborhood.”

This story underscores the truth of the observation that plastic surgeons can do almost anything with a nose except keep it out of other people’s business.

Eavesdropping, snooping, prying, looking are condemned in Jewish teachings that link human dignity to the right to privacy. After all, even God Himself is only partially revealed. Note that King David’s downfall began with his act of looking where he should not have been looking, at the bathing Bathsheba.

We are all aware that our privacy has eroded owing to concerns over security and through use and abuse of the Internet. As one author has it: “Modern Americans are so exposed, peered at, inquired about, and spied upon as to be increasingly without privacy, members of a naked society, and denizens of a goldfish bowl.”

While debates rage over the extent and limits of privacy, the sensitivity of the Torah to one’s right to privacy, affirming it as an important part of a person’s dignity, merits consideration.

– Rabbi Mark S. Miller is rabbi emeritus at Temple Bat Yahm in Newport Beach.
